\title{The rattly test mass: how large impulsive motion of a released test mass
corrupts a frequency-modulation OPD measurement, and a motion-robust estimator
that survives it\\
\large Physics of in-band injection, demonstration on FM1 Day09 data, and a
validated robust pipeline}
\author{HET IFO OPD analysis}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
In the frequency-modulation method a known single tone of the laser frequency
at $\fm$ writes a phase tone on the differential interferometer phase whose
amplitude is proportional to the residual optical path-length difference (OPD).
When the test mass is released it becomes a free harmonic oscillator, typically
resonating at a few hertz, and its motion is the science signal. This note is
about the single worst thing that can then happen to the measurement: a
\textbf{rattly} test mass, that is one whose motion is not a smooth pendulum
swing but an impulsive, repeatedly re-excited ring-down. We show that such
motion deposits broadband power directly inside the narrow demodulation band
around $\fm$, even though its mechanical fundamental sits far below $\fm$. The
reason is a large coupling asymmetry: the differential phase is
$\varphi=\nu(t)\,\OPD(t)/c$, so genuine OPD motion couples to phase through the
full optical frequency $\nuz$, whereas the wanted tone couples only through the
tiny modulation depth $\dnupk$. The in-band gain of motion over signal is
$\nuz/\dnupk\approx\gainratioround\times10^{6}$, so an OPD-motion component of a
few nanometres at $\fm$ already rivals the tone. We derive this effect, show it
in the FM1 Day09 released acquisitions (the near-tone noise floor of a rattly
system is $\floorasdratio\times$ that of a clean one), and explain why it drives
the standard incoherent estimator to over-report the OPD. We then implement and
validate a motion-robust pipeline: an absolute near-tone contamination floor and
reliability flag, a matched-filter coherent projection at the clock-locked tone
frequency, burst gating, and a high-resolution readout of the test-mass motion
from the low-frequency phase. On synthetic data with a known OPD the robust
estimator recovers the truth to within \synrobmaxerr\,\% at contamination levels
where the standard estimator is biased high by up to $\synstdmaxfac\times$. All
numbers and figures are regenerated from the raw data by \texttt{make\_figures.py}.
\end{abstract}

\section{The phenomenon}
\label{sec:phenom}

A released test mass in the measurement interferometer is a nearly free harmonic
oscillator. Its fundamental frequency is typically a few hertz, well below the
modulation tone $\fm=\fmod\,$Hz used in the FM1 campaign. During a released run
the mass is set in motion (by the release itself, by residual forces, or by
ground vibration) and rings. Two qualitatively different kinds of ring occur,
and they have completely different consequences for the OPD estimate:

\begin{enumerate}
\item A \textbf{clean} ring-down: a smooth, slowly decaying sinusoid at the
      mechanical fundamental. Its energy is concentrated at the fundamental and
      a few discrete harmonics, all far below $\fm$, and it dies away quietly. As
      we show below, this does not bias the OPD estimate at all, even when the
      swing is large (tens of cycles of differential phase).
\item A \textbf{rattly} ring-down: an impulsive, repeatedly re-excited motion
      (stick-slip, mechanical chatter, knocks). Its time series is choppy rather
      than sinusoidal, and, crucially, its spectrum has a broadband tail that
      reaches all the way up to and past $\fm$.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:ringdown} contrasts the two directly from FM1 Day09 data. Both
test masses were released and both moved by a comparable amount at low frequency
(peak-to-peak $\cleanptp$ cycles for the clean R1 case, $\rattlyptp$ cycles for
the rattly R2 case), so the difference that matters is not how far the mass
swung but the spectral character of the motion.

\section{Physics: why low-frequency motion lands in the tone band}
\label{sec:physics}

\subsection{The two ways motion enters the phase}

The differential interferometer phase (in cycles) is set by the instantaneous
optical frequency $\nu(t)$ times the instantaneous OPD, divided by the speed of
light,
\begin{equation}
  \varphi(t) = \frac{\nu(t)\,\OPD(t)}{c}.
  \label{eq:phi}
\end{equation}
Write the OPD as a static part plus motion, and the laser frequency as its
carrier plus the modulation,
\begin{align}
  \OPD(t) &= \OPD_0 + \delta(t), \\
  \nu(t)  &= \nuz + \dnupk\cos(2\pi\fm t).
\end{align}
Substituting into Eq.~\eqref{eq:phi} and keeping the four products,
\begin{equation}
  \varphi(t) =
  \underbrace{\frac{\nuz\,\OPD_0}{c}}_{\text{DC}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{\nuz}{c}\,\delta(t)}_{\text{motion, gain }\nuz/c}
  \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{\dnupk}{c}\,\OPD_0\cos(2\pi\fm t)}_{\text{wanted tone, gain }\dnupk/c}
  \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{\dnupk}{c}\,\delta(t)\cos(2\pi\fm t)}_{\text{tone AM}}.
  \label{eq:four}
\end{equation}
Two of these carry the motion $\delta(t)$, and it is essential to keep them
apart.

The \textbf{tone-AM term} (last) is what intuition expects: it multiplies the
motion by the tone, so a mechanical line at $f_{\text{mech}}$ appears as
sidebands at $\fm\pm f_{\text{mech}}$. For $f_{\text{mech}}\approx 7.7\,$Hz these
sidebands sit at $95\pm7.7\,$Hz, far outside the $\pm\demodbw\,$Hz demodulation
band, and are rejected. This term is not the problem.

The problem is the \textbf{second term}, $\nuz\,\delta(t)/c$. It is the motion
written straight onto the phase, with no shift in frequency. Any spectral content
of $\delta(t)$ that genuinely lies at $\fm\pm\demodbw\,$Hz lands directly inside
the demodulation band. It cannot be filtered out by narrowing the band, because
it is in the band.

\subsection{The million-fold gain asymmetry}

Compare the gains of the two motion-independent quantities we care about. The
wanted tone enters with gain $\dnupk/c$; the in-band motion enters with gain
$\nuz/c$. Their ratio is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\nuz}{\dnupk}
  = \frac{\nuzero\ \si{Hz}}{\dnupkval\times10^{6}\ \si{Hz}}
  \approx \gainratio .
  \label{eq:ratio}
\end{equation}
Here $\nuz=c/\lambda$ with $\lambda=\lambdanm\,$nm, and $\dnupk$ is the peak laser
frequency deviation of the modulation. So a motion component at $\fm$ that is only
about $10^{-6}$ of the low-frequency swing already rivals the signal tone.
Numerically, a mere $2\,$nm of $\delta\OPD$ content at $\fm$ reads as about
$\nmexample\,$mm of apparent OPD ($2\,\text{nm}\times\gainratio$). This is the
origin of the millimetre-scale spikes seen in contaminated records.

\subsection{So the demodulator's leakage immunity is real but specific}

The complex-baseband demodulator is immune to spectral leakage: it kills the
sidelobe leakage of strong out-of-band lines, so the large $7.7\,$Hz fundamental
and its low harmonics do not leak in through the discrete-Fourier-transform
window. That immunity is genuine and is why a clean ring-down (Section
\ref{sec:phenom}, case 1) is harmless however large. But no filter can remove
power that truly lies at $\fm\pm\demodbw\,$Hz. A rattly motion has exactly that:
a broadband tail with real power in the band, amplified by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ratio}.

\section{Evidence in the FM1 Day09 data}
\label{sec:evidence}

\subsection{The near-tone floor, not the swing amplitude}

Figure~\ref{fig:asd} shows the differential-phase spectra of the same clean and
rattly acquisitions. On the full spectrum both show the mechanical fundamental
and a harmonic comb. The decisive difference is local to the tone: zooming on the
demodulation band, the rattly system has a broadband floor
$\floorasdratio\times$ higher than the clean one
($\floorasdrattly$ versus $\floorasdclean$ cyc$/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ in the
$\fm\pm[0.6,1.5]\,$Hz flanks). This elevated in-band floor, amplified by the
$10^{6}$ factor of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ratio}, is what floods the estimator.

  systems show the few-hertz mechanical motion and harmonics. Right: zoom on the
  demodulation band (grey), where the rattly system's broadband floor is

\subsection{How the standard estimator is fooled}

The primary pipeline estimator (see the companion note on OPD estimation)
demodulates the tone to a complex baseband phasor $z(t)$ whose magnitude is the
instantaneous tone amplitude. It then chooses between a coherent estimate
(the vector mean $|\langle z\rangle|$, matched to a phase-stable tone) and an
incoherent estimate (the noise-debiased root-mean-square
$\sqrt{\langle|z|^2\rangle-S_n}$, matched to a wandering tone), based on a
measured coherence. In-band contamination breaks this logic in two ways:

\begin{enumerate}
\item It adds random-phase power to $z(t)$, which lowers the measured coherence.
      The selector reads this as a wandering tone and switches to the incoherent
      branch, even though the static OPD tone is not actually wandering.
\item The incoherent branch then reports
      $\sqrt{\langle|z|^2\rangle-S_n}$. Since the contamination adds to
      $\langle|z|^2\rangle$ and the single far off-tone reference under-estimates
      the coloured near-tone floor $S_n$, the branch sums the in-band motion
      power as if it were signal and over-reports the OPD.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:contam} shows this for the genuinely rattly Day09 R2 record
(NOSHIMS, torqued, RMS motion $\rattlymot\,\si{\micro m}$). The instantaneous
$|z(t)|$ wanders far above the coherent baseline; the standard incoherent
estimate ($\rattlystd\,$mm) sits high in that excursion, whereas the coherent
projection ($\rattlyrob\,$mm) tracks the lower baseline, and the near-tone floor
($\rattlyfloor\,\si{\micro m}$-equivalent) shows the in-band noise is comparable
to the tone itself. The separate R2 record that originally stood out with an
anomalously large OPD ($\outlierstd\,$mm) is an even more extreme case: its
near-tone floor is $\outlierfloor\,\si{\micro m}$-equivalent, comparable to the
apparent signal, so it is flagged (reliability $\outlierrelia$) rather than
trusted.

\section{A motion-robust pipeline}
\label{sec:robust}

The physics of Section~\ref{sec:physics} points to four concrete defences,
implemented in \texttt{het\_ifo\_opd.estimators.demodulate\_robust} and exposed
through \texttt{estimate\_opd\_robust}.

\subsection{An absolute near-tone contamination floor and a reliability flag}

Rather than one far off-tone reference at $\fm+7\,$Hz, the noise floor is
measured from several bins immediately flanking the tone (at
$\fm\pm\{1.2,1.6,2.0\}\,$Hz, whose passbands clear the tone) and combined by a
median. This gives the true coloured floor next to the tone, $S_n^{\text{adj}}$.
Two diagnostics follow: a relative contamination ratio
$S_n^{\text{adj}}/S_n^{\text{far}}$, and, more usefully, an absolute reliability
\begin{equation}
  \rho_{\text{rel}} = \frac{A_{\text{robust}}}{\sqrt{S_n^{\text{adj}}}},
\end{equation}
the ratio of the recovered tone amplitude to the near-tone noise amplitude. A
record with $\rho_{\text{rel}}\gtrsim\relthresh$ is trustworthy; a smaller value
flags a record where the motion-driven in-band noise is comparable to the tone
and the OPD cannot be certified from that acquisition.

\subsection{A matched-filter coherent projection at the clock-locked tone}

The static OPD produces a strictly constant phasor at exactly $\fm$ (the drive
and the phasemeter share a clock), so its optimal estimator is the vector mean
\begin{equation}
  A_{\text{robust}} = \bigl|\langle z(t)\rangle\bigr|.
\end{equation}
This is the matched filter for a constant, clock-locked tone. Any zero-mean
in-band contamination, which is what broadband motion power at $\fm$ looks like,
averages to zero in the vector mean, so the estimate is unbiased however large
that contamination is. Two points are important. First, the estimator does not
switch to the incoherent branch under low coherence, because here the low
coherence is contamination, not tone wander. Second, it deliberately avoids the
data-driven linear-phase-ramp de-rotation used by the default demodulator: a
phase corrupted by contamination corrupts that fit and would smear the true tone.
The tone frequency is fixed once, robustly, by the clock-locked refinement.

\subsection{Burst gating}

Rattly contamination arrives in bursts. An in-band noise witness (the magnitude
of an adjacent-band demodulation versus time) marks the quiet epochs, and the
coherent projection is formed over those epochs. When quiet epochs exist (as they
do between re-excitations of a ring-down) this sharpens the estimate and reduces
residual bias; when they do not, the reliability flag catches the record.

\subsection{The test-mass motion as a bonus}

Finally, the same physics that makes the in-band tail dangerous makes the
low-frequency phase an exquisite motion sensor. The second term of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:four} gives the displacement directly,
\begin{equation}
  \delta(t) = \frac{c}{\nuz}\,\varphi_{\text{LF}}(t) = \lambda\,\varphi_{\text{LF}}(t),
\end{equation}
where $\varphi_{\text{LF}}$ is the band-limited low-frequency differential phase
in cycles and one cycle equals one carrier wavelength of OPD. Laser frequency
noise is common-mode-rejected in the difference, so this is a clean, high
bandwidth readout of the released mass, reported alongside the OPD
(Figure~\ref{fig:motion}).

  of the rattly R2 acquisition. This is the force-sensing signal; the same

\end{figure}

\section{Validation on synthetic data with a known OPD}
\label{sec:synth}

Real records have no ground truth, so we validate the estimator against a known
injected OPD. The synthetic differential phase is a constant tone at $\fm$
encoding $\OPD_0=\synopdtrue\,$mm, plus a large low-frequency motion of several
cycles, plus a controlled random-phase narrowband contamination centred on
$\fm$ (this is exactly broadband motion power landing in the band with random
phase), plus white noise. We sweep the contamination level from zero to four
times the tone amplitude, in a bursty and in a steady variant.

Figure~\ref{fig:synth} is the central result. The standard estimator tracks the
truth at low contamination but, once the contamination exceeds the tone, it
switches to the incoherent branch and climbs well above the truth, reaching
$\synstdburst\,$mm (bursty) and $\synstdsteady\,$mm (steady) at four times the
tone, that is up to $\synstdmaxfac\times$ the true OPD. The robust estimator
stays on the truth throughout, recovering $\synrobburst\,$mm and
$\synrobsteady\,$mm respectively, and within \synrobmaxerr\,\% of the truth
across the whole sweep. This is the sense in which the estimator gets much better
at recovering the true OPD in the presence of large test-mass motion.

\section{The FM1 Day09 released acquisitions, before and after}
\label{sec:results}

Table~\ref{tab:day09} runs both pipelines on every Day09 released file.
The pattern is systematic. The R1 system rang cleanly: all four records are
coherent, their near-tone floors are small ($\floorRonemin$ to $\floorRonemax\,
\si{\micro m}$-equivalent), their reliabilities are well above $\relthresh$, and
the robust and standard OPDs agree. The R2 system rang rattly: every record is
pushed to the incoherent branch by in-band contamination, the near-tone floors
are an order of magnitude larger ($\floorRtwomin$ to $\floorRtwomax\,
\si{\micro m}$-equivalent), and three of the four are flagged as
low-reliability. For the genuinely rattly R2 record (NOSHIMS, torqued, RMS motion
$\rattlymot\,\si{\micro m}$) the standard estimate of $\rattlystd\,$mm collapses
to a flagged $\rattlyrob\,$mm once the summed motion power is removed. The R2
record that originally stood out as the large-OPD outlier is not silently
corrected but correctly flagged (reliability $\outlierrelia$), signalling that
its OPD is not trustworthy and the mass should be damped and the run repeated.

\section{Recommendations}
\label{sec:reco}

\paragraph{Experimental.} The cheapest and most effective fix is upstream: damp
the test mass before acquiring, and avoid impulsive re-excitation (gentle
release, isolate from knocks and stick-slip). A large but clean ring-down is
harmless; a small but rattly one is not. Where practical, record long so that
quiet epochs between re-excitations can be selected in analysis, and record the
mechanical spectrum so the broadband tail can be seen directly.

\paragraph{Analysis.} Use the robust pipeline for released runs. Always report
the near-tone floor and the reliability flag next to the OPD, and treat a flagged
record as an upper limit rather than a measurement. Report the recovered
test-mass motion as the force-sensing signal in its own right.
